Sim-to-Real VLA Pipeline for Mobile ALOHA

Role: Simulation Team Member and Environment Developer
Lab: Advanced Computation and Artificial Intelligence (ACAI) Lab, Dr. Shengfeng Yang
January 2026 to Present
Python MuJoCo dm_control LeRobot Jacobian-based IK VLA Models LLM Fine-tuning NSF ACCESS HPC Domain Randomization Digital Twin

Overview

Training Vision-Language-Action (VLA) foundation models for robotic manipulation requires massive amounts of demonstration data. Collecting this data physically is expensive, slow, and limits the diversity of scenarios the robot encounters. This project addresses that bottleneck with a comprehensive sim-to-real transfer pipeline built on the MuJoCo physics engine, generating thousands of diverse manipulation demonstrations for the 17-DOF Mobile ALOHA platform. The simulation provides multi-camera visual data, including overhead and wrist-mounted views, alongside 14-DOF physical effort feedback from all joints.

Figure 1: High-fidelity MuJoCo simulation of the 17-DOF Mobile ALOHA platform.

Figure 2: Left-arm camera visual observation data generated for VLA model training.

My contributions

  • Kinematics and Policy Development: Engineered four custom scripted policies for complex bimanual tasks, including tube-picking and mid-air handovers, using a Jacobian-based damped least squares Inverse Kinematics (IK) solver.
  • Advanced Manipulation Features: Implemented physical intelligence features into the control policies, including tube-position feedback to compensate for pendulum swing, grasp verification, and descent collision detection.
  • Domain Randomization: Developed a procedural system to generate randomized distractor objects across the workspace, varying object properties, positions, and environmental conditions to ensure the VLA model generalizes rather than memorizes a static scene.
  • Data Pipeline and Quality Control: Built a dense episode scoring system to evaluate and filter simulation runs on a scale from Perfect to Catastrophic, then export multi-modal data into LeRobot v3.0 format for model training.

Figure 3: Procedurally generated distractor objects added to the workspace for domain randomization.

Results

  • The pipeline generates large-scale, standardized datasets recording joint states, velocities, efforts, and action commands across thousands of randomized episodes.
  • Generated data is actively being used to fine-tune pre-trained VLA models via NSF ACCESS HPC resources, significantly reducing dependency on manual human teleoperation.
  • Fine-tuned VLA models and LLMs in Python to successfully automate bookshelf organization tasks on the real-world Mobile ALOHA platform.

EPICS MOBI: USA Blind Hockey Prototype

Role: Design Lead and Electrical Team Member
Engineering Projects in Community Service (EPICS), Mobility (MOBI), Purdue University
August 2024 to May 2025
Fusion 360 KiCad LTspice Polyurethane 3D Printing PCB Design Material Testing Structural Analysis

Overview

  • Project developed in partnership with USA Blind Hockey through the EPICS Mobility program at Purdue University.
  • Current blind hockey pucks use metal ball bearings for sound. They are heavy, frequently injure players, and break after a single game, making the sport unnecessarily expensive to play.
  • The objective was to design and prototype a reusable, safer puck that produces consistent sound via electronic components rather than physical bearings, while withstanding the demands of competitive play.

Figure 4: Early CAD iteration of the snap-fit puck design. The team ultimately pivoted to a threaded closure to improve durability and ease of maintenance.

Figure 5: The physical 3D-printed Polyurethane prototype, showing the internal cavity designed to securely house the electronic sound components.

My contributions

  • Directed a five-member team as Design Lead through the full project lifecycle, coordinating both the electrical and mechanical workstreams.
  • Electrical System Design: Developed the puck's internal electronics using KiCad for PCB layout and LTspice for circuit simulation. The primary challenge was designing the circuit to withstand severe impact forces without damaging the components.
  • Mechanical Design: Used Fusion 360 to conduct structural analysis and identify weak points in early models. Iterated from a snap-fit enclosure to a threaded and screwed closure, improving durability and simplifying battery replacement.
  • Geometry Optimization: Drop tests revealed that sharp angles created stress concentrations upon impact. Redesigned the geometry with curved edges and fillets to better distribute impact forces.
  • Material Selection: Evaluated PETG for early iterations and selected Polyurethane for the final prototype based on superior impact resistance, energy absorption, and performance at low temperatures.
  • Conducted real-user testing with a hockey player to validate mechanical durability, sound output consistency, and waterproofing under game conditions.

Results

  • The final Polyurethane prototype withstood 500 N of force during drop testing while maintaining waterproofing and consistent sound output.
  • Delivered a functional proof-of-concept for USA Blind Hockey demonstrating a safer, reusable, multi-game puck. Future development will focus on refining internal component mounting and preparing the enclosure for scalable manufacturing.
Final prototype validated: 500 N impact resistance, consistent sound output, waterproof, tested with a real athlete.

3DEXPERIENCE Digital Twin: Dual-Piston Mechanism

Purdue University Milestone Course, Dassault Systemes
Tools: CATIA, 3DEXPERIENCE (Part Design, Assembly Design, Creative Experience), Apple Vision Pro
CATIA 3DEXPERIENCE Apple Vision Pro Part Design Assembly Design Kinematics AR/VR Integration Digital Twin

Overview

  • The objective of this project was to design a functional mechanical assembly from the ground up and translate it into an interactive digital twin for Augmented Reality (AR) visualization.
  • Using the Dassault Systemes 3DEXPERIENCE platform, a complete dual-piston mechanism was modeled and prepared for immersive simulation on Apple Vision Pro.
  • Components designed include a manual hand-crank wheel, a two-gear power transmission system, a main crankshaft, connecting rods, two piston heads, and a solid cylinder block.

Figure 6: The fully constrained dual-piston assembly in CATIA, featuring the hand-crank input, gear train, and cylinder block.

My contributions

  • Component Modeling: Engineered all individual components using the Part Design application within 3DEXPERIENCE.
  • Assembly and Motion: Established mechanical relationships between parts in the Assembly Design environment. Applied precise joint constraints including revolute joints for the gear train and cylindrical constraints for the pistons, ensuring the assembly possesses realistic kinematic motion. Turning the hand wheel accurately drives the gears, which in turn rotates the main rod and cycles the pistons inside the block.
  • AR Integration: Used the Creative Experience application to deploy the fully constrained kinematic assembly to the Dassault Systemes application on an Apple Vision Pro headset, enabling users to view the digital twin in 3D space, interact with the hand wheel, and analyze the internal motion of the piston-cylinder mechanism in real time.

Results

  • Demonstrated the complete lifecycle of a digital twin: from foundational CAD modeling and kinematic assembly to immersive AR visualization on Apple Vision Pro.
  • The workflow has direct industrial applications including risk-free operator training, synthetic kinematic data generation for predictive maintenance models, and pre-manufacturing design validation in a mixed-reality environment.

Little Brazer Engine Assembly

Purdue University, MFET 164
Tools: Siemens NX, Teamcenter (PLM), Bottom-Up Assembly Modeling
Siemens NX Teamcenter Bottom-Up Assembly Modeling PLM Geometric Constraints Version Control

Overview

  • Designed and assembled a functional mechanical model of a Little Brazer engine using Siemens NX and Teamcenter, following industry-standard CAD and Product Lifecycle Management (PLM) workflows.
  • Individual components include the engine body, cooling cylinder, crankshaft, cam, connecting rod, and wrist pin brackets, all modeled from scratch.

Figure 7: The Little Brazer Engine mid-assembly in Siemens NX, highlighting the strict geometric constraints applied to the components to ensure proper kinematic motion.

My contributions

  • Part Design: Engineered all individual mechanical components from scratch using Siemens NX.
  • Assembly and Constraints: Performed a bottom-up assembly of the engine, applying rigorous geometric constraints including concentric, distance, and touch constraints to ensure the crankshaft and piston rod articulate correctly without mechanical interference.
  • Data Management: Used Teamcenter to manage CAD files, handle version control, and maintain the structural integrity of the sub-assemblies throughout the design process, mirroring industry-standard PLM practices.

Results

  • Produced a fully constrained, kinematically valid engine assembly, demonstrating the ability to design individual parts for manufacturability, manage sub-assemblies with version control, and validate kinematic accuracy entirely within a PLM environment before physical production.

The Nostradamus Project: Global Hurricane Predictor

Purdue University, ENGR 133 | Role: Creator and Developer
September 2024 to December 2024
Python Pandas Folium Geocoding APIs NOAA Historical Datasets Geospatial Mapping Data Analysis

Overview

  • A Python-based analytical tool that processes over 180 years of historical hurricane data from 1840 to the present, sourced from the National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI).
  • The program calculates and visualizes the probability of a hurricane occurring in any user-defined city worldwide during a specific month, translating raw meteorological data into an accessible risk estimate.

Figure 8: An interactive Folium heatmap generated by the program, visualizing the global frequency of hurricanes in October. The map features a personalized risk calculation marker placed in Miami, Florida.

My contributions

  • Geocoding and User Input: Implemented an API workflow that takes a user's text input, searches the web, and converts it into precise latitude and longitude coordinates.
  • Data Processing: Engineered the backend using the Pandas library to clean, filter, and analyze massive NOAA datasets. The algorithm matches historical hurricane coordinate IDs to the user's localized search radius to calculate a precise probability percentage.
  • Geospatial Visualization: Used the Folium library to generate an interactive, navigable global heatmap. The program drops a customized marker at the user's exact location, complete with a tooltip that translates the complex data into a clear, readable risk percentage.

Results

  • The program accurately calculates and visualizes hurricane probability for any city worldwide, processing over 180 years of NOAA data in a navigable interactive map.
